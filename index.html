Breathing

Smoke

How crop burning in Northern India chokes Delhi every winter

By Ishayu Ghosh, Shreya Bharathwajan, and Annabelle Guiditta

wipe the smoke or scroll

What is the problem?

Every year, Delhi disappears into smog.

Delhi and the broader National Capital Region are frequently ranked among the most polluted major urban areas in the world. For people who live there, the pollution is not an abstract environmental issue. It is something that settles over roads, homes, schools, markets, and lungs.

In a city of more than thirty million people, toxic air turns ordinary routines into daily exposure. The haze can blur the skyline, dim the sun, and make breathing feel heavy even before anyone checks an air quality number.

Long-term exposure to polluted air is linked to a significant reduction in life expectancy. Even healthy residents can suffer from chronic headaches, itchy eyes, coughing, and respiratory issues. This section begins with the simplest question: before looking at fires, farms, and data, what does it mean for a city to live under air that is unsafe to breathe?

A window that’s too small

After their rice harvest, farmers in Punjab and Haryana have only a few weeks to prepare their fields and sow wheat. The gap leaves little time — and for many farmers, little choice — to clear what is left behind.

Punjab and Haryana are regions of India north-west of Delhi. They are among India’s largest producers of rice and wheat, feeding millions across the country. But after rice is harvested in late October, farmers have only a very small window — often just 7 to 10 days — to clear their fields before wheat sowing begins in early November.

Clearing crop residue quickly manually takes time and money that many farmers cannot afford. Missing the sowing window can reduce yields dramatically, cutting into already thin profits. For many farmers, burning the leftover stubble becomes the fastest and cheapest way to prepare fields in time.

At the beginning of the season, fire detections remain relatively scattered across northern India. The MODIS satellite data captures all thermal anomalies -- not every fire is confirmed crop burning -- but large clusters have not yet formed across Punjab and Haryana.

The region is entering the dry post-monsoon season, when harvested fields begin clearing and vegetation moisture drops. Smoke exists, but the landscape has not yet shifted into the concentrated burn period tied to wheat preparation.

As the rice harvest finishes and farmers rush to plant wheat, fires begin appearing more frequently across Punjab and Haryana.

At the same time, northwesterly winds begin pushing smoke southeast toward Delhi and the Indo-Gangetic Plain, carrying pollution far beyond the fields where the fires originate.

The pattern becomes difficult to ignore.

By this stage, daily detections can exceed 1,500 fires in a single day. Distinct clusters now dominate parts of Punjab and Haryana, aligning closely with the narrow window farmers face between rice harvest and wheat sowing.

The burn season reaches its maximum intensity.

Daily detections climb above 3,400 fires with more than 10,000 fires already recorded within the six days prior. The concentration of activity across Punjab and Haryana becomes one of the largest seasonal fire bursts visible in the region each year.

Smoke continues to follow wind routes into Delhi, blocked by the Himalayas which form a barrier, preventing air from dissipating.

After the sowing window passes, the fire counts begin falling almost as quickly as they rose.

The MODIS dataset still captures many kinds of fires across northern India, but the intense concentration visible during the peak period rapidly weakens. What remains is a clear seasonal pattern: a short, explosive burst of fire activity that appears irregular compared to surrounding weeks and repeats during the post-harvest transition into winter.

What reaches Delhi?

The smoke does not stop at the farms where it begins.

During the post-monsoon season, northwesterly winds carry pollution from Punjab and Haryana toward Delhi, where it mixes with emissions already trapped within the city: traffic, construction dust, coal power, industry, and waste burning.

By the time the smoke arrives, Delhi's air is often already polluted. Seasonal burning does not create the crisis alone -- it intensifies one that already exists.

How polluted is Delhi?

Delhi is regularly ranked among the most polluted major cities in the world.

In 2023, average PM2.5 concentrations in the city reached roughly 100 micrograms per cubic meter -- more than 20 times higher than the World Health Organization's recommended annual limit. During severe winter smog events, pollution levels can rise several times higher still.

Why winter makes it worse

Winter turns Delhi into a basin for trapped air.

During colder months, cool dense air settles near the ground while warmer air forms a layer above it -- a phenomenon known as temperature inversion. Instead of rising and dispersing, pollutants become trapped close to breathing height.

Fog makes the problem even worse. Moist winter air combines with pollutants to create dense smog capable of covering entire sections of the city for days.

Delhi's geography compounds the effect. The city sits within the Indo-Gangetic Plain, with the Himalayas to the north acting as a partial barrier that limits air circulation and slows dispersal.

The smoke is only part of it

Stubble burning is one contributor to Delhi's pollution crisis, but not the only one.

An estimated 1.1 million vehicles enter and leave Delhi every day. Coal power plants surrounding the city release sulphur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and soot into the atmosphere. Construction dust, industrial emissions, and landfill fires all add to the pollution already hanging over the region.

During winter, these sources combine together beneath the same stagnant air.

Layered emissions visualization: vehicles, coal plants, construction dust, landfill fires, and agricultural smoke all feeding into the same haze layer over Delhi.

During winter, the numbers become visible.

For much of the year, Delhi's pollution exists as statistics: PM2.5 readings, satellite detections, mortality estimates, air quality indexes.

But during winter, the numbers become visible. Horizons disappear into haze. Schools close. Flights are delayed. Masks return to crowded streets. More than thirty million people continue daily life beneath air that health experts routinely classify as unsafe to breathe.

What begins as smoke over harvested fields hundreds of kilometers away eventually settles into the atmosphere of an entire city.
