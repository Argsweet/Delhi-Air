What is the problem?

Every year, Delhi disappears into smog.

Delhi and the broader National Capital Region are frequently ranked among the most polluted major urban areas in the world. For people who live there, the pollution is not an abstract environmental issue. It is something that settles over roads, homes, schools, markets, and lungs.

In a city of more than thirty million people, toxic air turns ordinary routines into daily exposure. The haze can blur the skyline, dim the sun, and make breathing feel heavy even before anyone checks an air quality number.

Long-term exposure to polluted air is linked to a significant reduction in life expectancy. Even healthy residents can suffer from chronic headaches, itchy eyes, coughing, and respiratory issues. This section begins with the simplest question: before looking at fires, farms, and data, what does it mean for a city to live under air that is unsafe to breathe?

A window that’s too small

After the harvest, farmers have only a few weeks to prepare their fields and sow wheat. The gap leaves little time — and for many farmers, little choice — to clear what is left behind.

Punjab and Haryana are among India’s largest producers of rice and wheat, feeding millions across the country. But after rice is harvested in late October, farmers have only a very small window — often just 7 to 10 days — to clear their fields before wheat sowing begins in early November.

Clearing crop residue manually takes time and money that many farmers cannot afford. Missing the sowing window can reduce yields dramatically, cutting into already thin profits. For many farmers, burning the leftover stubble becomes the fastest and cheapest way to prepare fields in time.

Before the window closes, the map is quiet.

At the start of the season, detected fires are scattered. There are fires, but the pattern has not yet concentrated across the northwest.

Then the count begins to climb.

As the short rice-to-wheat turnaround arrives, fire detections begin clustering across Punjab and Haryana.

The boom is sudden.

The total fire count peaks over a narrow period, matching the pressure farmers face to clear fields quickly before wheat sowing.

Afterward, the fires taper off.

Once the planting window passes, new detections decline. The burst does not last all season; it rises, concentrates, and fades.

This is a pattern, not a perfect label.

Total fire count does not mean every detected fire is crop-residue burning. But when the biggest boom appears right around the 7–10 day field-clearing window, the timing suggests a strong seasonal pattern.
