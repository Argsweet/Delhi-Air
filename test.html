The Why

A window
that's too small

After rice harvest, farmers have only a 7–10 day window to prepare their fields and sow wheat. The short gap leaves no time—and no choice but to burn what's left behind.

Punjab and Haryana together produce over 50% of India's wheat and rice. A missed sowing window means a lost season — for farmers already earning less than $3/day, that's catastrophic.

October–November

After rice harvest,
fires ignite

Each year between October and November, satellite data from MODIS detects a dramatic spike in fire activity across Punjab and Haryana — the breadbasket states upwind of Delhi.

Peak: Oct 25 – Nov 10

Thousands of fires
in days

At peak season, over 4,000 active fires are detected in a single day across the two states — a 40× increase from baseline levels in September.

Wind Direction

Smoke travels
straight to Delhi

Prevailing north-westerly winds during this period carry the smoke plume directly over Delhi. The city has no escape — hemmed in by the Himalayas to the north and the Aravalli ridge to the south.

Year after year

It happens
every season

Despite bans and subsidies for alternative methods, the pattern repeats annually. Farmers face ₹2,500 fines — but clearing a hectare by machine costs ₹6,000. The economics make burning inevitable.

Year-round baseline

Delhi air is
never truly clean

Even in summer, Delhi's PM2.5 levels average 60–80 µg/m³ — four to five times the WHO safe limit of 15 µg/m³.

October–November

Crop burning
breaks the scale

When fires peak, PM2.5 regularly breaches 500 µg/m³ — 33× the safe limit. Visibility drops to under 500 metres. Flights are grounded. Schools close.

Health burden

A health crisis
hiding in plain sight

Long-term exposure at these levels cuts life expectancy by an estimated 8–12 years for Delhi residents. Children born today will breathe this air for decades.

What needs to change

Subsidise
the alternative

Happy Seeder machines can plant wheat directly into stubble — but cost ₹1.5 lakh to rent per season. Without meaningful subsidies, burning will remain the only viable option for smallholder farmers.
